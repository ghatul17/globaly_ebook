Moving to a new country comes with a long to-do list, and opening a local bank account is usually near the top. Without one, you're paying foreign transaction fees on every purchase, can't receive local salary payments, and often can't set up utilities or rent a flat.

The good news: with the right preparation, it's very doable.

## What You'll Typically Need

Most banks — regardless of country — ask for some combination of:

- **Valid passport** (and sometimes a secondary ID)
- **Proof of address** — a utility bill, rental contract, or official letter dated within the last 3 months
- **Proof of income or employment** — a contract, recent payslips, or a letter from your employer
- **Tax identification number** — your home country's TIN or the local equivalent
- **Visa or residency permit** — showing your legal right to be in the country

The exact requirements vary significantly by country. Germany requires an Anmeldung (registered address). The UAE requires an Emirates ID. The UK is relatively flexible if you have a National Insurance number.

## Digital Banks Are Often Easier

If you're in the early weeks of your move and don't yet have a local address, traditional banks can be frustrating. **Digital banks are usually the faster route**:

- **Wise** — multi-currency account, works in 40+ countries, no local address required
- **Revolut** — fast to open, good for travel spending
- **N26** — works well across Europe
- **Monzo** — UK-focused but strong for day-to-day banking

These won't replace a full local bank account for everything (some landlords and employers insist on a "proper" local bank), but they're a great bridge while you get settled.

## Tips to Make It Easier

1. **Get your address registered first** — even a temporary one. Many countries have a formal address registration process (Germany's Anmeldung, France's titre de séjour). Banks follow the paper trail.

2. **Bring originals AND copies** — some banks want certified copies; some want originals plus photocopies. Bring both to avoid a second trip.

3. **Ask your employer first** — if you're relocating for work, your employer may have a preferred banking partner and can smooth the process considerably.

4. **Check for expat-friendly branches** — in cities with large expat populations, certain bank branches handle international customers regularly and staff know what documents to expect.
